\section{The Light-Clock Derivation and the Geometry of Rotation}

The transverse light-clock provides the fundamental geometric connection, with roots in Einstein's original 1905 paper. For mirrors with proper separation $d$ moving at velocity $v$, light travels along the hypotenuse:
\[
(c\Delta t)^2 = d^2 + (v\Delta t)^2.
\]
Solving yields:
\[
\Delta t = \frac{d}{\sqrt{c^2 - v^2}} = \frac{d}{c\sqrt{1-\beta^2}} = \gamma\Delta\tau,
\]
where $\Delta\tau = d/c$ is the proper time and $\beta = v/c$.

This admits two geometric interpretations that reveal the deep connection to complex and hyperbolic geometry \cite{Yaglom1968, Needham1997}:

\begin{definition}[Circular Interpretation]
Define $\cos\theta = \sqrt{1-\beta^2} = 1/\gamma$, $\sin\theta = \beta$. Then:
\[
\Delta t = \frac{\Delta\tau}{\cos\theta}.
\]
The factor $\gamma$ appears as a secant, suggesting a projection effect familiar from complex analysis \cite{Ahlfors1979}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Hyperbolic Interpretation]  
Define rapidity $\phi$ via $\tanh\phi = \beta$. Then:
\[
\cosh\phi = \gamma, \quad \sinh\phi = \gamma\beta, \quad \Delta t = \Delta\tau\cosh\phi.
\]
This reveals the boost as a hyperbolic rotation in spacetime, extending the geometric view of \cite{Misner1973}.
\end{definition}

The hyperbolic interpretation is fundamental: \emph{Lorentz boosts are rotations in Minkowski space}. The rapidity $\phi$ serves as the natural hyperbolic angle, with velocity composition corresponding to angle addition: $\phi_{\text{total}} = \phi_1 + \phi_2$.
